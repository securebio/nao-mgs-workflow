Specific Qs about proper processing of Ultima reads:
- Is read trimming required? Yes. Ultima sequencers have an onboard "Ultima Trimmer" that often handles adapter removal before you even see the data. However, if you are working with raw FASTQs, standard adapter trimming is required. Quality trimming is also recommended, but keep in mind that Ultima's quality scores are generated by a neural network predicting homopolymer lengths, so they represent a probability of length accuracy rather than a pure substitution probability.
- Length filtering and masking low-entropy reads? This is critical for Ultima data.Length: With ~300bp reads, filtering out fragments that become too short post-trimming (e.g., < 50bp) is standard.Entropy: Because homopolymer indels are the main error type, low-complexity regions and long homopolymer tracts will cause massive amounts of spurious alignments. Aggressively masking low-entropy reads (using tools like dust or the entropy filter in bbduk) will save you from a flood of false-positive viral hits.
- K-mer methods (Kraken, BBDuk)? Standard $k$-mer methods are highly vulnerable to indels because a single inserted or deleted base in a homopolymer breaks every overlapping $k$-mer. Therefore, default Illumina parameters will lead to significant dropouts and unclassified reads with Ultima data.Kraken: You will likely need to build or use a database with a smaller $k$-mer size (e.g., $k=21$ to $25$ instead of the default $35$) to improve classification sensitivity.BBDuk: When using bbduk for positive or negative filtering, you must allow for edits (e.g., edits=1 or 2). Strict exact-match $k$-mer filtering will erroneously discard true positive viral reads simply because they contain a slight homopolymer miscall.
- Preferred mappers (Bowtie2 vs. Minimap2)? Minimap2 and BWA-MEM are preferred. BWA-MEM handles indels much more gracefully out of the box (in fact, Ultima’s proprietary onboard "Ultima Aligner" is a derivative of BWA). Minimap2 (using the -x sr preset for short reads) is also excellent because it was designed from the ground up to handle indel-heavy data.Bowtie2 is heavily optimized for substitution-prone Illumina reads and strongly penalizes gaps by default. If you use Bowtie2, you must explicitly relax the gap penalties (--rdg and --rfg); otherwise, your mapping rates will plummet.

Overall summary of differences between Illumina and Ultima:
1. Sequencing Chemistry & Error ProfilesYour assumption about error profiles is completely correct. Neither is inherently "worse," but they require entirely different statistical models to correct.Illumina (Sequencing by Synthesis): Uses reversible terminators (adding one base at a time).Error Type: Dominated by base substitutions (SNVs). Indels are incredibly rare.Position Bias: Errors gradually increase toward the 3' end of the read due to phasing/pre-phasing noise as the sequencing cycles progress.Ultima (Flow-based / Mostly Natural SBS): Flows one nucleotide type (A, C, T, or G) over the flow cell at a time and measures how many incorporate.Error Type: Extremely low SNV errors, but dominated by indels in homopolymer regions (e.g., struggling to tell if a sequence is AAAAAAA or AAAAAAAA).Position Bias: Error rates spike sharply in homopolymers longer than 10bp, and general accuracy degrades after ~200bp into the read.2. Read Structure & LayoutIllumina: Paired-end and fixed-length (e.g., 2x150bp). Because reads are fixed, aligners and structural variant callers rely heavily on the expected insert size between the two paired reads to map across repetitive regions.Ultima: Single-end and variable-length (ranging from ~50bp to >300bp, averaging around 300bp). Because there is no paired read, bioinformatics tools must rely solely on the longer continuous read length for mapping and structural variant calling.3. Data Formats, Quality Scores, & Trimming"Flow Space" vs. "Sequence Space": Illumina outputs traditional Phred quality scores for each discrete base. Ultima calculates quality scores in "flow space" (the probability of how many bases were incorporated during a specific nucleotide flow).File Formats: Ultima natively outputs highly compressed CRAM files directly from the instrument containing these flow-space metrics, bypassing traditional FASTQ generation.Trimming Differences: Because Ultima reads are variable length, adapter trimming must be entirely dynamic (you cannot just trim the last $X$ bases). Furthermore, in applications like single-cell RNA-seq, Ultima's flow-based chemistry degrades when reading through long Poly(T) tails. Bioinformaticians often have to trim/shorten the UMIs (Unique Molecular Identifiers) in Ultima data to maintain downstream data quality.4. Downstream Pipelines & Variant CallingSpecialized Callers Needed: You cannot use standard variant callers (like standard GATK) optimally on Ultima data. You must use machine learning models—specifically, customized versions of Google's DeepVariant—that have been explicitly trained to interpret Ultima's flow-space data and penalize homopolymer errors.The "High Confidence Region" (HCR): Because Ultima struggles with extreme homopolymers and shows coverage dropouts in highly GC-rich regions, their whole-genome variant calling is often measured against an "Ultima High Confidence Region" (HCR). This mask excludes roughly 4% to 10% of the trickiest parts of the genome that Illumina can typically sequence through.
