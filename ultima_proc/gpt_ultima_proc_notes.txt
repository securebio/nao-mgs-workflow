Specific Qs about proper processing of Ultima reads:
3) Trimming: yes, but lighter and more library-aware than “just do Illumina trimming.”

Some trimming is still appropriate. Ultima’s official RNA-seq application note removed adapters, did quality trimming, trimmed reads longer than 120 bp down to 120, filtered reads shorter than 50 bp, and then aligned with STAR. Ultima’s bioinformatics page also describes a platform trimmer that handles read segments, adapters, and barcodes, and says RNA-seq output is intended for downstream tools such as STAR.

But I would not blindly port an Illumina trimming recipe. In one independent UMGC benchmark, the first ~70 bases were Illumina adapters introduced by the converted library design and were removed before analysis; that is a study-specific artifact, not a universal property of native Ultima libraries. For your wastewater RNA data, I’d trim known adapters/constant regions, trim poly(A/T) if the library structure can generate them, and then do only light quality trimming where cycle-quality or motif-specific QC shows a problem. BBDuk supports poly-A/T trimming, entropy filtering, and quality trimming directly.

4) Length filtering / low-entropy filtering: mild short-read QC, not ONT-style aggressive filtering.

I would not do ONT-style aggressive minimum-length filtering just because the data are Ultima. A mild minlen after trimming is sensible; 50 bp is consistent with the official Ultima RNA-seq note and common BBDuk preprocessing. Kraken 2 also cannot classify sequences shorter than its k-mer length, which is 35 by default. Keep low-complexity handling enabled: BLAST uses DUST for nucleotide low-complexity masking, Kraken 2 masks low-complexity sequences during database build by default, and BBDuk can do entropy masking/filtering plus poly-A/T trimming.

So my answer is: yes to mild length/entropy QC, no to ONT-style heavy filtering. For wastewater RNA, the things worth removing are obvious junk reads, short post-trim fragments, poly(A/T) tails, and simple low-complexity artifacts.

5) K-mer methods: still appropriate, but exact-match loss will hurt a bit more than on Illumina.

Yes, k-mer/minimizer methods are still appropriate for Ultima. Kraken 2 remains a standard fast first-pass classifier; its defaults are k=35 and minimizer length 31, it uses low-complexity masking in DB construction, and --confidence lets you trade sensitivity for conservatism by pushing labels up the tree or leaving reads unclassified. Official and independent RNA-seq results suggest Ultima is still very much in the short-read regime, with broadly comparable downstream RNA-seq results to Illumina.

What changes is that localized homopolymer/context errors can knock out runs of overlapping exact k-mers. An early independent Ultima scRNA-seq analysis reported elevated errors in and near homopolymers and found pseudoalignment more forgiving than standard alignment; newer Nature Methods work on flow-based Ultima data still shows explicit homopolymer-size and flowspace-context error structure. That does not mean k-mer methods stop working. It means I would expect a little more recall loss than with Illumina in those motifs, especially for read-level positive filtering.

For Kraken 2, I would first tune database composition and --confidence, not immediately rebuild with shorter k. If you do experiment with shorter k, do it only against a spike-in benchmark because you are changing the sensitivity/specificity balance. For BBDuk, I’d absolutely keep using it for adapter removal and small contaminant sets. BBDuk itself recommends adapter settings around k=23 mink=11 hdist=1, supports separate short-kmer distance settings (hdist2), and offers mcf as a more stable match criterion. For very large positive viral-reference sets, BBDuk is a less natural choice, because its own docs note that mismatch/edit-distance settings scale steeply in memory; that is one reason I’d use BBDuk mainly for adapters, polyA/T, rRNA k-mers, or small curated panels, and use Kraken2 or mapping for broad viral retention.

6) Preferred mappers: Bowtie2 for depletion is the safest default; minimap2 is viable; STAR for spliced host RNA.

For host depletion, Bowtie2 is the safest current starting point. A recent viral/microbial metagenomics benchmark found that Bowtie2 --very-sensitive-local in single-end mode with the T2T-CHM13 human reference best balanced host-read removal and preservation of microbial signal.

For mapping to viral genomes, both Bowtie2 and minimap2 are reasonable. Minimap2 explicitly supports accurate short reads with -ax sr, now also supports short-read RNA-seq with -ax splice:sr, and its documentation states it is as accurate as Bowtie2/BWA on simulated >100-bp short reads while being faster. Bowtie2, though, still gives very familiar short-read local/sensitive behavior and has better current benchmark support for the specific host-depletion problem. For spliced host-transcriptome/genome alignment, STAR remains the official Ultima RNA-seq recommendation, even though minimap2 now has a short-read spliced preset and Heng Li still describes STAR as the de facto standard for short RNA-seq alignment.

My short version would be: Bowtie2 for human depletion and viral recruitment, STAR for spliced host RNA, and minimap2 as a good fast alternative for genomic-like short-read mapping if you benchmark it on your data.


Overall summary of differences between Illumina and Ultima:
The most useful bioinformatics mental model is this: Illumina data are usually fixed-length, paired-end, base-space reads with standard phred-like per-base quality scores and very mature default tooling; Ultima data are flow-based, commonly single-end and variable-length, and they often work best with platform-aware preprocessing and calling. Both end up in familiar formats like FASTQ/CRAM, but they are not interchangeable at the error-model level.

The first big difference is read layout. Illumina commonly gives you paired-end reads, and the mate information helps alignment in repetitive regions and supports indels, rearrangements, fusions, and isoform discovery. Ultima UG100 workflows, by contrast, are described as single-read/flow-based, with read lengths distributed around the ~250–300 bp range rather than a fixed 2×150 layout; for some single-cell workflows, those single-end reads are explicitly converted into simulated paired-end FASTQs so tools like Cell Ranger can consume them.

The second big difference is error behavior. Illumina errors are classically modeled as per-base errors that rise toward the end of the read and show sequence-context effects; that is exactly the sort of behavior mainstream short-read aligners and callers have been tuned around for years. Ultima’s flow-based chemistry changes the failure mode: the signal reflects how many identical bases were incorporated in a flow, so the hard cases are more about homopolymer-length estimation and other flow-space/context-specific effects than ordinary base substitutions. A 2025 Nature Methods paper on Ultima data explicitly highlights homopolymer-size estimation and “cycle shift motifs” as distinctive features of the error profile.

That leads to the third difference: variant calling pipelines. With Illumina, a generic “short-read best practices” stack is usually the default assumption. With Ultima, Broad added a dedicated --flow-mode to GATK’s HaplotypeCaller and Mutect2, and Ultima’s own workflow docs emphasize supplementary quality tags plus UG-adapted DeepVariant/DNAscope/GATK models. In practice, that means Ultima data usually deserve a platform-aware aligner/caller path, not a blind drop-in Illumina pipeline.

A subtle but important benchmarking point is that accuracy numbers are not always directly comparable across platforms unless the truth regions are matched. Ultima’s own high-confidence region excludes areas where performance is weaker, including homopolymers longer than 12 bp and some low-complexity regions. Recent 2025–2026 preprints also report remaining context-specific errors in UG100 and note that some clinically relevant variants fall outside the UG HCR, so when comparing Illumina and Ultima variant calls you should compare them on the same benchmark regions, not on platform-specific masks.

For single-cell RNA-seq, the data can be quite comparable biologically, but the differences are very bioinformatics-visible. The Nature Biotechnology benchmark found Ultima and Illumina scRNA-seq were broadly similar, but minor differences came from the fact that Ultima’s single-end reads sit closer to gene ends than Illumina’s paired-end reads, which affects assignment relative to annotated gene boundaries. The same paper describes explicit trimming/splitting rules to convert Ultima single-end reads into paired-end-like inputs, and it notes a current preference for 5′ over 3′ Ultima libraries because quality near the poly(T) region can reduce usable reads in 3′ assays.

For paired-end-dependent assays, Illumina still has a practical advantage. The same scRNA-seq paper notes Ultima was not used for 10x TCR/BCR libraries because those constructs required paired-end reads or substantially longer single-end coverage to span the full sequence. More generally, Illumina’s paired-end design is naturally helpful for fusions, novel isoforms, and rearrangement-style analyses.

For bulk RNA-seq, the picture is usually friendlier to Ultima than for small-variant calling. An independent University of Minnesota benchmark on 97 samples reported slightly lower UG100 base qualities than Illumina, but strong gene-expression correlations between platforms after trimming, with the caveat that the comparison was restricted to Illumina R1 versus Ultima’s forward reads because UG100 is single-end. So for gene-level counting, the platforms can be quite similar downstream, while transcript-level tasks that benefit from mate information still lean Illumina.

So the practical summary is: Illumina is the lower-friction choice when your pipeline assumes mate pairs and standard short-read error models; Ultima is attractive when throughput/cost matter and your application is tolerant of single-end flow-based data or has a validated Ultima-aware workflow. Gene counting and many large-scale digital assays can be very comparable. Small-variant calling in homopolymer/low-complexity sequence, V(D)J, fusions, and other paired-end-heavy analyses are where the differences matter most.
